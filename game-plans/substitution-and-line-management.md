# 🥍 Substitution & Line Management

**Video Reference:** [Lacrosse Substitutions - How and When](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=nn0dpQ2yWoE)

## 🎯 Overview

System for managing midfield lines, attack/defense rotations, and special-teams transitions during live play. Effective substitution management keeps legs fresh, prevents empty-net miscounts, and ensures the right personnel are on the field for every situation. This guide covers horn signals, bench protocols, and situational personnel decisions that win close games in the fourth quarter.

**Target Audience:** Coaches / All Players
**Focus Area:** Game Management

## 💡 Key Concepts

1. **Horn System:** Use a designated horn blast or whistle pattern to signal line changes. A single long horn means the current midfield line sprints off and the next line enters. The bench coach controls the horn — players never sub themselves. This removes confusion and keeps the pace coach-driven rather than player-driven.
2. **Midfield Rotation (3 Lines of 3):** Organize your midfield into three lines of three players each, rotating every 2–3 minutes of game clock. Line 1 is your top unit for critical possessions; Line 2 and Line 3 should each have a defined identity (e.g., Line 2 is your transition line, Line 3 is your grinding/defensive line). Fresh legs in the second half are a massive competitive advantage — teams that rotate well outrun opponents in Q4.
3. **Attack Stays On Unless Injury or Foul:** Your three attackmen remain on the field for the entire half whenever possible. Limit attack substitutions to dead-ball situations only — injuries, penalties, or the halftime break. Constant attack subs disrupt offensive chemistry and create transition vulnerabilities when the sub box is open.
4. **LSM/SSDM Swap Rules:** Bring in the Long Stick Middie (LSM) on every defensive possession when possible, subbing out the weakest short-stick middie from the current line. If you are in a fast-break or unsettled situation, keep the short-stick middies on — the LSM subs in on the next dead ball. Short-Stick Defensive Middies (SSDMs) enter when you need a lockdown matchup on a specific opposing attackman or middie but still want stick skills in transition.
5. **Foul Management:** Track team fouls by quarter using a wristband card or whiteboard. If a player picks up two personals in a half, consider pulling them proactively before they foul out. Communicate foul counts to players on the field — a defender with two fouls should play more conservatively and avoid reaching checks. Losing a starter to an unnecessary third foul in a close game is a coaching failure, not a player failure.

## ⚙️ Setup

- **Substitution Box Protocols:** The sub box is the 10-yard area at midfield on the table side. Only the players entering and exiting may be in the box during a live sub. Players exiting must be fully off before the entering player crosses the line — simultaneous overlap causes a too-many-men penalty. Drill this in practice until it is second nature.
- **Coach Hand Signals:** Establish a simple signal system visible from the bench. Examples: one finger up = Line 1, two fingers = Line 2, three fingers = Line 3, fist pump = LSM in, open palm = stay on. The bench coach relays these signals and verbally confirms with the next line.
- **Bench Organization:** The next midfield line must always be standing, sticks in hand, kneeling at the sub box gate, ready to go before the horn sounds. Remaining lines sit in order on the bench. Goalies, extra attackmen, and face-off specialists have designated spots. A disorganized bench creates 5–10 seconds of delay on every sub — that is an eternity in a live game.

## 🏃 Execution

### Phase 1: Standard Rotation — Dead-Ball Subs & Horn-Triggered Midfield Changes

1. On every dead ball (after a goal, out-of-bounds, or penalty), the bench coach evaluates whether a line change is needed based on time-on-field and upcoming game situation.
2. Horn sounds once — current midfield line sprints to the box, next line enters immediately. The sub should take no more than 3–4 seconds.
3. Attack and close defense remain on the field unless the coach signals a specific personnel change (injury replacement, matchup adjustment).
4. The face-off middie always enters for face-offs regardless of line rotation. He exits for the designated middie from the on-field line once possession is established.

### Phase 2: On-the-Fly Subs — During Clears, Rides, and Matchup-Based Switches

1. During a clear from the defensive end, the midfield line on the field can be swapped on-the-fly as the ball crosses midfield. The exiting middies peel off to the box while the new line enters from the table side.
2. On rides (after a turnover or save), keep the current line on unless they have been on for 3+ minutes. Tired middies riding is worse than fresh middies not riding — fatigue leads to blown assignments and easy clears.
3. Matchup-based switches: if the opposing team subs in a specific player (e.g., their top dodger), the bench coach can call in a designated defender (LSM or SSDM) to counter, regardless of rotation.

### Phase 3: Situational — Man-Up/Man-Down, End-of-Half, and Fatigue-Based Pulls

1. **Man-Up (EMO):** When the opponent takes a penalty, immediately sub in your designated EMO unit. This is a pre-set group — typically your best shooters and feeders plus one middie who can carry from up top. They enter on the dead ball before the penalty is served.
2. **Man-Down (MDD):** When your team takes a penalty, sub in the MDD unit immediately — your toughest, most disciplined defenders and the LSM. Remove any offensive-minded middies who are not part of the MDD package.
3. **End-of-Half/Game Personnel:** With under 2 minutes in any half, get your best line on the field. If protecting a lead, insert your most reliable ball-handlers and defenders. If trailing, go with your top scoring line and most aggressive middies.
4. **Fatigue-Based Pulls:** The bench coach should track shifts. Any middie who has been on for 4+ minutes without a sub gets pulled at the next opportunity — even mid-possession on a dead ball. Exhausted players make poor decisions, lose ground balls, and get beaten on defense.

## 🧠 Strategic Mindset

- **Fresh Legs Win Q3/Q4 🦵:** The team that manages energy better in the second half wins the close games. Your Line 3 players must be ready to play significant minutes in Q4 — they are not backups, they are closers. Rotate honestly all game so no single line is gassed when it matters.
- **Scout the Opponent's Fatigue 👀:** Watch for opposing middies who are bent over, jogging back on rides, or losing foot races they won in Q1. Call out their tired numbers to your offensive middies — attack the player who has been on the field the longest.
- **Communicate "Next Line" Before the Whistle 🗣️:** The bench coach should announce the next line 30 seconds before the expected sub. The next three middies stand, confirm their assignment ("Line 2, we're in on the next dead ball"), and move to the gate. No one should be surprised when the horn blows.

## ✅ Desired Outcomes

- No empty substitutions — the team never plays with fewer than 10 on the field due to a sub-box miscommunication.
- No confusion at the box — every player knows exactly when they are entering and who they are replacing.
- Every player knows their line assignment and their role within that line (who dodges, who rides hard, who covers the fast break).
- Midfield shifts average 2–3 minutes, keeping legs fresh through the final quarter.
- Man-up and man-down units enter within one dead ball of the penalty call — no wasted possessions with the wrong personnel.

## 📋 Coaching Points

- **Assign a Bench Coach to Manage the Box:** The head coach should not be managing subs and calling plays simultaneously. Designate an assistant whose sole job on game day is tracking shifts, calling lines, and managing the horn. This is the most important non-head-coach role on the staff.
- **Use Wristband Cards for Lines:** Every player wears a wristband card listing the three midfield lines, the EMO unit, and the MDD unit. If a player ever forgets their line, they check their wrist — no excuses, no asking the bench mid-game.
- **Practice Subs in Scrimmages:** Dedicate 5 minutes of every full scrimmage to live substitution reps. Blow the horn mid-play, force on-the-fly subs, and penalize the team (push-ups, sprint) for any too-many-men violations. Game-day sub execution is only as good as your practice habits.
- **Track Fouls on a Whiteboard:** Keep a visible tally of personal fouls by player number on a small whiteboard at the bench. When a player hits two fouls in a half, the bench coach notifies the head coach immediately for a pull decision.
- **Dead-Ball Sub Discipline:** Emphasize that players do not sub themselves. No player leaves or enters the field without the bench coach's signal. Self-subbing creates chaos and miscounts — enforce this rule strictly from day one.
