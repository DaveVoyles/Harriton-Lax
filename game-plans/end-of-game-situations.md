# 🥍 End-of-Game Situations

**Video Reference:** [Special Situations | End Game Double and Stall | POWLAX](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=00qhJN8dG8U)

## 🎯 Overview

A comprehensive guide for managing critical late-game scenarios — stalling offense, last-possession plays, protecting a lead, and comeback attacks. Games are won and lost in the final 4 minutes, and players must have rehearsed responses for every situation so decisions are automatic, not emotional.

**Target Audience:** All Players
**Focus Area:** Game Management, Decision Making

## 💡 Key Concepts

1. **Stall Offense Principles:** Once the stall warning is called (or when protecting a lead in the final 2 minutes), the offense shifts to ball movement, clock awareness, and the 2-pass rule — never hold the ball for more than 2 passes without moving it. The goal is to eat clock while staying in the attack area. Dodge only to reset, never to force a low-percentage shot.
2. **Last-Possession Attack Strategy:** When down by 1 with less than 2 minutes remaining, the team must execute a called set play. Identify the best matchup, clear out space, and attack with urgency but not panic. The ball carrier should dodge to score or draw a slide and dish to the open man.
3. **Protecting a Lead:** When up by 1 or 2 in the final minutes, the defensive unit shifts to a conservative posture — ride hard on clears, play prevent defense (no gambling for takeaways), and avoid fouls at all costs. Every possession matters; clearing under pressure is paramount.
4. **Face-Off Implications:** In late-game situations, winning the face-off can be the difference. Communicate with the face-off specialist about strategy — whether to go for a clean win or a controlled tie-up to burn clock.

## ⚙️ Setup

- **Personnel for Stall Sets:** Your best ball-handlers and dodgers should be on the field. Prioritize players who protect the ball well and make smart decisions under pressure.
- **Formation:** Use a spread 2-2-2 or 1-4-1 high set to maximize spacing and force the defense to cover the entire attack area. Wide spacing makes it harder for defenders to double-team.
- **Sideline Communication Protocols:** Coaches must relay clock information loudly and clearly. Designate one coach as the "clock coach" who calls out time remaining at 30-second intervals. Captains on the field echo the calls.

## 🏃 Execution

### Phase 1: Stalling with Possession

1. Move the ball constantly — swing it side to side, use skip passes to reverse the field.
2. Dodge only to reset your position or draw a slide, never to force a shot unless you have a clean look.
3. Stay in the attack area at all times once a stall warning is active.
4. Use the 2-pass rule: after receiving the ball, make at least 2 passes before considering a shot.

### Phase 2: Down by 1 — Aggressive Attack

1. Call a specific set play from the sideline (e.g., "Lightning" or "X-iso").
2. Identify the best individual matchup and clear space for a 1v1 dodge.
3. Attack with urgency — the dodger must get to the middle of the field for the best scoring angle.
4. Off-ball players cut hard to create passing lanes if the slide comes.

### Phase 3: Up by 1 — Protect the Lead

1. Ride hard on every clear — pressure the ball and deny easy outlets.
2. Play sound defense — stay in your slides, no gambling for takeaways.
3. Do NOT foul — a man-up opportunity for the opponent is the worst possible outcome.
4. If you get the ball, run stall offense (see Phase 1).

## 🧠 Strategic Mindset

- **Clock Awareness ⏱️:** Every player must know the score and time remaining. Decisions change based on game state — a 2-goal lead with 3 minutes left is different from a 1-goal lead with 30 seconds.
- **Composure Under Pressure 🧊:** Late-game situations reward calm, rehearsed responses. Panic leads to turnovers, bad shots, and fouls. Trust the plays you have practiced.
- **Trust Your Sets 🔗:** The time to improvise is NOT with 90 seconds left in a 1-goal game. Execute the play as designed and trust your teammates to be in the right spots.

## ✅ Desired Outcomes

- Players have rehearsed late-game responses for every common scenario (up 1, down 1, tied, stall warning).
- No panic decisions in the final 4 minutes — every action is intentional and practiced.
- Clock management is second nature; players instinctively know when to attack and when to possess.
- The team converts at least 50% of last-possession situations into quality scoring opportunities.

## 📋 Coaching Points

- **Practice Stall Offense Weekly:** Dedicate 10 minutes every practice to running stall sets. Players must be comfortable moving the ball without forcing.
- **Situation Scrimmage:** Use "situation scrimmage" in practice — set the score and clock to simulate late-game scenarios (e.g., "Down 1, 90 seconds left, your ball").
- **Captain Responsibilities:** Captains must know the clock rules cold — stall warning rules, timeouts remaining, and when to call specific plays.
- **Common Mistake:** Players rush shots under stall pressure. Reinforce that a stall violation is better than a bad turnover that leads to a fast break goal against.
