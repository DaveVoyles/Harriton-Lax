# 🥍 Defensive Double Teams

**Video Reference:** [How To Execute A Proper Defensive Double Team | Lacrosse All Stars](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=J_7i_tG8SmU)

## 🎯 Overview

A comprehensive guide for executing defensive double teams in lacrosse — when to trap, how to rotate, and how to recover. Covers GLE doubles, crease doubles, and pick-and-roll traps. Mastering the double team turns aggressive defense into a turnover-generating weapon.

**Target Audience:** Defense, LSM
**Focus Area:** Communication, Rotation, Aggression

## 💡 Key Concepts

1. **Trap Triggers — When to Double:** Look for weak hand, back to goal at GLE, after a pick, or when the shot clock is low. These are the moments when the ball carrier is most vulnerable and least likely to make a clean pass out of pressure.
2. **Communication Before the Trap:** The sliding defender must call "double! double!" before arriving. The on-ball defender hears the call and adjusts to take away the primary escape pass. No call, no double.
3. **Rotation of Remaining 4 Defenders:** The four off-ball defenders collapse to protect the crease, deny the most dangerous pass, and recover inside-out. Think of it as a diamond shell that shrinks around the goal.
4. **Recovery After the Forced Turnover or Pass:** Once the ball carrier passes or turns it over, sprint back to your man. Recovery is always inside-out — get to the crease first, then find your man.

## ⚙️ Defensive Setup

- **Who Doubles:** The adjacent defender or the crease defender, depending on ball location. The on-ball defender's job is to force the attacker into a spot where the trap can arrive.
- **GLE Doubles:** The crease defender slides to trap the ball carrier at GLE while the adjacent defender rotates down to cover the crease. This is the most common double in settled defense.
- **Topside Doubles:** The adjacent defender and the on-ball defender work together to wall off the ball carrier. The crease defender stays home to protect the most dangerous area.
- **4-Man Rotation Shell:** The remaining four defenders form a diamond shape protecting the crease. Deny inside passes, force the ball to the perimeter, and recover inside-out when the ball moves.

## 🏃 Execution

### Phase 1: The Trap

1. The on-ball defender forces the attacker to a predetermined spot (GLE, sideline, or after a pick).
2. The second defender arrives on the stick-side to take away the pass and shrink the ball carrier's space.
3. Both defenders mirror their sticks — one high, one low — to eliminate passing lanes.

### Phase 2: 4-Man Shell

1. The remaining four defenders shift to a box/diamond shape around the crease.
2. Priority is denying inside passes — force the ball to skip over the top where recovery time is longer.
3. Communicate constantly: "I've got crease!" "I've got two!" "Skip! Skip!"

### Phase 3: Forced Pass or Turnover

1. If the ball carrier forces a pass, the shell defenders recover to their man inside-out.
2. The closest defender to the new ball carrier closes out under control.
3. Reset the defense — do not chase the ball after the double is broken.

### Phase 4: Transition Push

1. If the double creates a turnover, push transition immediately.
2. The player who caused the turnover becomes the ball carrier and looks to outlet.
3. Sprint to numbers — the goal is to create an odd-man advantage before the opposing defense sets up.

## 🧠 Strategic Mindset

- **Commitment 💯:** Doubles are a gamble — commit fully or don't go at all. A half-hearted double is the worst defensive play in lacrosse. Two players caught in no-man's land is worse than no slide at all.
- **Communication 🗣️:** Every double must be called before it happens. "Double! Double!" is the trigger. If you don't hear the call, don't leave your man.
- **Trust 🤝:** Trust your teammates to rotate behind you. The double only works when all six defenders are connected. One player freelancing breaks the entire scheme.

## ✅ Desired Outcomes

- Players recognize trap opportunities in real-time without coaching prompts
- Rotation behind the double is automatic and fluid
- Double teams create 2+ turnovers per game
- Recovery after a broken double is fast and organized

## 📋 Coaching Points

- **Practice in 6v6 settings:** Doubles must be repped in game-like scenarios, not just shell drill.
- **Start with walk-through rotations:** Walk before you run — teach the shell first, then add speed.
- **Film review doubles every week:** Watch your doubles on film to identify breakdowns and celebrate successes.
- **Never double without calling it:** Make this an absolute rule. Silent doubles lead to blown coverages and easy goals.
