# 🥍 Defensive Communication Glossary

## 🎯 Overview

Defense is communication. The most athletic team in the world will get carved apart if they play in silence. This glossary is the complete reference for every defensive call in our system. Every player — from the goalie to the first middie back — must know these terms, when to use them, and what to do when they hear them. Print this out. Put it in your bag. Quiz each other.

**Target Audience:** All Players (Goalies, Defense, Midfield)
**Focus Area:** Communication, Defensive Coordination, Team Defense

## 💡 Key Concepts

1. **Talk is the First Slide:** A well-timed call prevents the dodge before it starts. If you call out the pick early enough, your teammate can fight over it without needing help.
2. **Volume and Clarity:** A mumbled call is the same as no call. Every call must be **loud**, **short**, and **repeated**. Say it once for yourself, say it twice for your teammates.
3. **The Goalie is the Defensive Quarterback:** The goalie sees the whole field. His calls override all others. When the goalie speaks, everyone listens.
4. **No Silent Defenders:** If you don't have a specific call to make, you should still be talking — "I see ball," "I'm in the hole," "I've got your back." Constant chatter keeps the unit connected.

---

## ⚙️ Tactical Setup

### How to Use This Glossary

- **In Practice:** Coaches should enforce every call on this list during team defense reps. If a player is silent, stop the rep and make them say the call before continuing.
- **On Game Day:** Print the Quick Reference Card (at the bottom) and give it to players during warm-ups as a reminder.
- **Film Review:** When reviewing defensive breakdowns, the first question is always: "What call was missed?" Assign the missed call to the responsible player.

## 🏃 Execution Principles

### Phase 1: On-Ball Calls

These calls are made by the defender guarding the ball carrier and his nearest teammates.

| Call | When to Use It | Who Says It | Expected Response |
|------|---------------|-------------|-------------------|
| **"Ball! Ball! Ball!"** | When you are the on-ball defender. | The defender guarding the ball carrier. | All other defenders identify their man and adjust positioning. The nearest defender calls "Hot." |
| **"I got ball!"** | When a new defender picks up the ball carrier after a switch or rotation. | The defender taking over on-ball responsibility. | The previous on-ball defender peels off to the hole or recovers to an adjacent man. |
| **"Check!"** | When the ball carrier catches a pass and hasn't moved yet — the window to apply immediate pressure. | The on-ball defender or the goalie. | The on-ball defender closes out aggressively with a stick check, choking the dodging lane before the ball carrier can get his hands free. |
| **"Topside!"** | When the ball carrier is above GLE and the on-ball defender should force him down toward the baseline. | Adjacent defenders or the goalie. | The on-ball defender positions his body to take away the topside lane, funneling the dodger toward the endline and help defense. |
| **"Hold!"** | When the on-ball defender is in good position and no slide is needed — the dodger is contained. | The on-ball defender himself. | Slide defenders stay home on their men. No one leaves their assignment. |

---

### Phase 2: Slide Calls

These calls coordinate the [sliding and recovery](sliding-and-recovery.md) system. They determine who helps, when, and where.

| Call | When to Use It | Who Says It | Expected Response |
|------|---------------|-------------|-------------------|
| **"Hot! Hot! Hot!"** | You are the designated first slide — the next man to help if the on-ball defender gets beat. | The defender closest to the ball carrier's dodging lane (typically the crease defender). | The "hot" defender is ready to leave his man and slide to the ball carrier's hands the moment the on-ball defender is beaten. |
| **"Fire!"** | The on-ball defender is beaten and the slide must go NOW. | The goalie or the on-ball defender. | The "hot" defender commits fully to the slide. No hesitation. Sprint to the ball carrier's hands with stick up. The "two" defender rotates to cover the crease. |
| **"2-Slide!" / "I'm two!"** | You are the second slide — the defender responsible for covering the man left open when the "hot" defender slides. | The next closest defender to the crease area. | The "two" defender sinks toward the crease to pick up the open man. He does not chase the ball — he protects the house. |
| **"Coma!"** | A slide is needed from the crease area specifically — the ball carrier has gotten to GLE or below and the crease defender is the only viable help. | The goalie or the adjacent defender. | The crease defender slides to the ball. The backside defenders collapse to protect the crease area. This is a high-risk call — recovery must be immediate. |
| **"No slide! No slide!"** | The on-ball defender has recovered or the ball carrier has pulled out — the slide is no longer needed. | The goalie or the on-ball defender. | All defenders stay home on their assignments. Reset positioning and re-establish the "hot" and "two" designations. |

---

### Phase 3: Off-Ball Calls

These calls come from defenders away from the ball and maintain the integrity of the defensive shell.

| Call | When to Use It | Who Says It | Expected Response |
|------|---------------|-------------|-------------------|
| **"Dead side!"** | Your man is on the opposite side of the field from the ball — low threat. | The defender whose man is farthest from the ball. | That defender sags toward the hole (crease area) to provide depth and potential help, while keeping his man in peripheral vision. |
| **"Deny!"** | Your man is one pass away from the ball and in a dangerous position — do not let him catch it. | The defender on a potential receiver near the ball. | That defender plays tight, gets a hand or stick in the passing lane, and forces his man to go backdoor or away from the ball. |
| **"Get in!" / "Get in the hole!"** | A defender is too far from the crease and needs to provide more interior help. | The goalie or an adjacent defender. | The addressed defender sinks to within 5 yards of the crease, shrinking the defensive shell and clogging passing lanes to the inside. |
| **"Pick left!" / "Pick right!"** | An offensive player is setting a screen on the specified side. | The defender who sees the pick coming (any off-ball defender). | The on-ball defender hears the call and fights over/under the pick. The defender guarding the picker prepares to switch, hedge, or re-route. See [2-Man Game defense](../offense/mastering-the-2-man-game.md) for pick defensive responses. |
| **"Switch!"** | Two defenders are changing assignments, typically after a pick or screen. | Both defenders involved in the switch, confirmed by the goalie. | Each defender takes the other's man cleanly. Communication must be instant — the moment of the switch is the offense's best window. |
| **"Stay!"** | After a pick, the defenders will NOT switch — each stays with their original man. | The defender guarding the picker, or the goalie. | The on-ball defender fights through the pick. The defender guarding the picker "shows" (steps out briefly to slow the ball carrier) and then recovers to his own man. |

---

### Phase 4: Clearing and Transition Calls

These calls are used during the transition from defense to offense — after saves, turnovers, and ground ball wins. They connect to our [Clearing Strategy](../rides-and-clears/clearing.md).

| Call | When to Use It | Who Says It | Expected Response |
|------|---------------|-------------|-------------------|
| **"Break!"** | A fast break opportunity is on — we have numbers and should push. | The goalie after a save, or any player after a turnover. | All players sprint to fill their transition lanes. Middies get wide. Attack checks away from defenders. The ball moves up-field immediately. |
| **"Outlet!"** | The ball carrier is under pressure and needs a safe passing target. | The nearest open teammate. | That player shows himself to the ball carrier — gets to an open lane with hands up, ready to receive. |
| **"Slide right!" / "Slide left!"** | During the clear, directing the ball to move toward the specified side of the field. | The goalie or the defensive coordinator on the sideline. | The ball moves to the indicated side. Players on that side create space. Players on the opposite side clear out of the way. |
| **"Settle!"** | There is no fast break — bring the ball up under control and organize into a set. | The goalie, the ball carrier, or the on-field leader. | Everyone stops sprinting and moves to their settled positions. The tempo drops. The offense calls a formation. |

---

### Phase 5: Situational Calls

These calls are triggered by specific game situations.

| Call | When to Use It | Who Says It | Expected Response |
|------|---------------|-------------|-------------------|
| **"Time!"** | The shot clock or game clock is running down and the offense must be forced into a bad shot. | The goalie, or any defender who can see the clock. | The on-ball defender increases pressure. Off-ball defenders tighten their marks. The goal is to force a contested outside shot as time expires. |
| **"Stall!"** | The offense is holding the ball and trying to run clock (usually when leading). | The goalie or the defensive leader. | Defenders play patient, disciplined defense. Don't lunge or get drawn into a bad slide. Force the offense to make a decision — they're under more pressure than we are. |
| **"Ride!"** | After a turnover or a shot, the team should pressure the opposing clear instead of falling back. | The midfield leader or the coaching staff. | Midfielders and attack apply immediate pressure to the opposing goalie and defenders. Match up and deny outlets. See [Riding Strategy](../rides-and-clears/riding.md). |
| **"Match up!"** | After a dead ball, face-off, or substitution — everyone needs to find and communicate their defensive assignment. | The goalie. | Every defender points to their man and says the jersey number aloud: "I've got 22!" Ensures no one is left uncovered. |
| **"Man down!"** | We are playing with fewer players due to a penalty. | The goalie or the defensive leader. | The defense shifts into the [5-Man Rotation](man-down/5-man-rotation.md). Communication must increase because there are fewer bodies to cover more space. |

---

### Phase 6: Goalie-Specific Calls

The goalie is the eyes of the defense. These calls are exclusive to or primarily driven by the goalie.

| Call | When to Use It | Who Says It | Expected Response |
|------|---------------|-------------|-------------------|
| **"Check!"** | A pass is completed to an offensive player — the nearest defender must pressure immediately. | Goalie. | The closest defender to the new ball carrier closes out with stick up, taking away the shooting lane. |
| **"Clear!"** | After a save — the goalie has the ball and wants to start the clear. | Goalie. | All defenders and middies release to their clearing lanes. The goalie looks for the outlet pass within 2 seconds. |
| **"Fire!"** | The on-ball defender is beaten and the first slide must go immediately. | Goalie. | The "hot" defender slides without hesitation. The goalie may also direct the "two" defender to fill. |
| **"You're hot!" / "You're two!"** | Assigning slide responsibilities as the ball moves around the offensive set. | Goalie. | The addressed defender acknowledges ("Got it!" or "I'm hot!") and positions himself to slide if needed. |
| **"One more!"** | The ball carrier is one pass away from a dangerous position (e.g., moving the ball behind the cage). | Goalie. | All defenders shift and re-establish positioning. The goalie is telling the defense to stay alert — the next action could require a slide. |
| **"Who's got [number]?"** | The goalie sees an uncovered offensive player. | Goalie. | The nearest defender picks up the open man immediately. This call prevents easy back-door goals. |

## 🧠 Strategic Mindset

- **Repetition Builds Habit 🔣:** These calls need to become automatic. In practice, players should be talking so much that it feels like too much. In a game, the noise and adrenaline will cut communication in half — so practice at 200% so game day is at 100%.
- **Communication 🗣️:** The standard is simple: if you are on defense and your mouth is closed, you are wrong. Every player should be making at least one call every 3-5 seconds during a defensive possession.
- **Spacing 📐:** Communication and spacing are linked. When a defender calls "dead side" and sags to the hole, the defensive shell tightens automatically. When a defender calls "deny," the shell stretches. The calls dictate the shape.
- **Accountability 🏋️:** In film review, missed calls should be identified and assigned. "The slide was late" is not the diagnosis — "no one called Fire" is the diagnosis. Fix the communication and the slides fix themselves.

## ✅ Desired Outcomes

- Every defensive possession features continuous communication — zero silent reps in practice or games.
- All slide assignments ("hot," "two") are verbally confirmed before the ball carrier initiates a dodge.
- Picks and screens are called out at least 2 seconds before they arrive, giving the on-ball defender time to react.
- Clearing calls are immediate and decisive — "Break" or "Settle" within 2 seconds of a change of possession.
- Goalie commands are followed without hesitation. The goalie's call is the final word.

## 📋 Coaching Points

- **Silent Rep Penalty:** If a defensive rep in practice features no communication, run it again. Every time. Players will learn that talking is non-negotiable.
- **Call Quizzes:** Randomly point at a player during practice and ask: "What's your call right now?" They should be able to answer instantly based on ball position and their role.
- **Goalie Leadership:** The goalie must be the loudest player on the field. Dedicate time in practice to goalie communication reps where the goalie directs the defense without playing the ball.
- **Film Focus — Audio:** When reviewing film, turn UP the audio. Can you hear the calls? If the field is silent, that's the first thing to fix.
- **Pair New Players with Veterans:** New players learn communication by standing next to someone who does it well. In early-season reps, pair every underclassman with a vocal upperclassman.
