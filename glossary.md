# 🥍 Lacrosse Glossary

## 🎯 Overview

A comprehensive glossary of lacrosse terminology for Harriton High School players and coaches. Use this as a quick-reference guide during film sessions, practice planning, and pre-game preparation.

**Target Audience:** All Players and Coaches
**Focus Area:** Terminology, Communication, Game Awareness

---

## 💡 Key Concepts

This glossary is organized into categories so you can quickly find the term you need. Every player should be fluent in these terms — communication wins games.

---

## ⚙️ Positions & Personnel

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **Attack (A)** | The three primary offensive players who stay on the offensive half. Responsible for scoring and feeding. |
| **Close Defense (D)** | The three primary defenders who match up against the opposing attack. Typically carry long poles. |
| **FOGO** | Face-Off Get-Off. A specialist midfielder whose sole job is to win the face-off and then substitute out. |
| **Goalie (G)** | The goalkeeper. Directs the defense, makes saves, and initiates clears. The quarterback of the defense. |
| **LSM (Long Stick Midfielder)** | A midfielder who carries a long pole (52–72 inches). Plays primarily on the defensive end and is often the first slide. |
| **Midfield (M)** | The three midfielders who play both ends of the field. Responsible for transition and two-way play. |
| **SSDM (Short Stick Defensive Midfielder)** | A midfielder with a short stick who specializes in defense. Often matched up against the opposing team's best midfield dodger. |

---

## 🏃 Field Areas & Landmarks

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **Alley** | The narrow lanes along the sidelines between the restraining line and the end line. Used for dodges and feeds. |
| **Box** | The area directly in front of the goal defined by the goal line extended and the top of the restraining box. The primary scoring zone. |
| **Crease** | The 9-foot radius circle around the goal. Only the goalie may be inside the crease. Offensive players cannot enter. |
| **GLE (Goal Line Extended)** | An imaginary line extending from the goal line to the sideline on each side. A critical reference point for angles and positioning. |
| **Restraining Line** | The line 20 yards from each end line. Only four offensive and three defensive players (plus the goalie) may cross it until possession is gained on a face-off. |
| **The Hole** | The area directly in front of the crease. The most dangerous offensive real estate on the field. Also called "the doorstep." |
| **The House** | The rectangular scoring area roughly 10 yards wide and 15 yards deep in front of the goal. High-percentage shooting zone. |
| **The Island** | The area behind the goal, extending 5–7 yards. An isolation zone for 1v1 dodges from X. |
| **Wing** | The areas on either side of the midfield line where wing players line up for face-offs. Also the sideline areas used in transition. |
| **X (Behind the Cage)** | The position directly behind the goal. A primary initiation point for offensive sets and feeding. |

---

## 🧠 Dodge Types

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **Bull Dodge** | A power move where the dodger drops a shoulder and drives through the defender using strength and low pad level. Best for bigger, stronger players. |
| **Face Dodge** | A change-of-hands fake where the player pulls the stick across their face without switching hands, then accelerates past the defender. No hand switch required. |
| **Question Mark Dodge** | A dodge from X where the attacker drives up the side of the goal, plants, and curls back toward the cage — tracing a "?" shape. Creates time and space for a shot or feed. |
| **Roll Dodge** | The dodger plants the lead foot, spins away from the defender while switching hands, and seals the defender on the back hip. Effective when the defender overcommits to one side. |
| **Split Dodge** | A change-of-direction move where the dodger plants hard, switches hands, and explodes past the defender in the opposite direction. The most fundamental dodge in lacrosse. |

---

## 🛡️ Slide Terminology

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **2-Slide (Two)** | The second defender responsible for rotating to cover the player left open by the first slide. Provides depth behind the hot slide. |
| **3-Slide (Three)** | The third defender in the rotation chain. Responsible for backside coverage and preventing skip passes. |
| **Adjacent Slide** | A slide package where the defender nearest to the ball carrier (not the crease defender) is the first to slide. Common on topside dodges. |
| **Crease Slide** | A slide package where the crease defender is always the first to leave and help on the ball. Creates a quick, aggressive first slide but leaves the crease temporarily open. |
| **Hot** | The designated first-slide defender. The player who is "hot" must be in a loaded position, ready to leave their man and help on a dodge. Called out verbally: "I'm hot!" |
| **Coma Slide** | A slide package where the help defender is positioned in the "coma" — a deep, off-ball position far from the dodger. The slider has a long distance to cover but protects the backside. |
| **String** | The concept of defensive connectivity — staying in a "string" or line between the ball and the goal. If the string breaks, the defense is vulnerable. |
| **Backer** | The second-slide defender in a defensive rotation. Provides depth behind the first slide and prevents easy passes to the crease. Also called "two." |

## 🏹 Offensive Concepts

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **Clear** | Transitioning the ball from the defensive half to the offensive half after gaining possession. The goalie and defense work together to advance the ball. |
| **EMO (Extra Man Offense / Man-Up)** | The offensive unit that plays when the opposing team has a player in the penalty box. Typically a 6v5 advantage. Also called "Man-Up." |
| **Fast Break** | An odd-man advantage in transition (e.g., 4v3 or 3v2) created by pushing the ball up the field quickly before the defense can set up. |
| **Motion** | Continuous off-ball player movement designed to create confusion, force defensive switches, and open cutting lanes. |
| **Pick (Screen)** | An off-ball action where an offensive player positions their body to block a defender's path, freeing a teammate for a dodge, shot, or receiving position. |
| **Secondary Break** | The window of opportunity after the initial fast break is stopped but before the defense is fully settled. Typically a 5v4 or 6v5 look in semi-transition. |
| **Settled Offense** | A 6v6 half-court offensive set where both teams are organized. Requires patience, ball movement, and execution of designed plays. |
| **Invert** | An offensive alignment where attackmen and midfielders swap traditional positions — attackmen play up top while midfielders play behind the cage. Forces long-pole defenders into uncomfortable positions. |
| **Skip Pass** | A pass that "skips" over one or more adjacent players to reach a teammate on the far side of the field. High-risk, high-reward — devastating when the defense over-rotates. |
| **Draw-and-Dump** | The ball carrier draws a slide defender, then passes ("dumps") to the player left open by the slide. The foundation of 2-man game offense. |
| **Quick Stick** | A catch-and-shoot in one motion. No cradling. Requires soft hands and pre-shot preparation. Deadly on feeds to the crease. |
| **Feeding** | The act of passing the ball to a teammate in a scoring position, typically from behind the cage (X) or the wing. Requires deception, timing, and vision. |
| **One-More** | The offensive philosophy of making one additional pass beyond the first open look to create a higher-percentage scoring opportunity. "The first open look is good; the second is great." |
| **Wheel** | A play where an attackman behind the cage (X) drives upfield while a midfielder curls behind and replaces at X, creating continuous motion and defensive confusion. |
| **Brush Pick** | A subtle, legal screen where an offensive player grazes a teammate's defender while moving through the offense. Less obvious than a full pick, harder to defend. |

## 🔄 Defensive & Transition Concepts

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **MDD (Man-Down Defense)** | The defensive unit that plays when your team has a player in the penalty box. Typically a 5v6 disadvantage. Also called "Man-Down." |
| **Man-Ball** | The defensive principle of maintaining visual awareness of both your assigned man and the ball at all times. Head on a swivel. |
| **Ride** | Defensive pressure applied during the opposing team's clear attempt. The goal is to force turnovers or burn the shot clock in the offensive half. |
| **Recovery** | The act of sprinting back to a defensive position after a slide, a turnover, or getting beaten on a dodge. Recovery lanes should always go through "the hole." |
| **Box-and-One** | A hybrid defensive scheme where 5 defenders play a zone (box shape around the crease) while 1 defender face-guards the opponent's best player man-to-man. |

---

## 🤼 Face-Off Terminology

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **Clamp** | The most common face-off move. The FOGO drives the back of the stick head over the ball, trapping it underneath. Requires wrist strength and low pad level. |
| **Plunger** | A face-off move where the FOGO pushes the ball forward through the opponent's stick, popping it out the back side. Effective against bigger opponents. |
| **Rake** | A face-off move where the FOGO pulls the ball backward toward their own body, then directs it to a wing player. Quick and deceptive. |
| **Motorcycle Grip** | The standard face-off grip where the top hand is over the throat of the stick (like gripping a motorcycle throttle). Provides maximum leverage for clamps. |
| **Over** | A face-off win directed to the offensive side (the wing closer to the opponent's goal). Typically the preferred direction. |
| **Under** | A face-off win directed to the defensive side (the wing closer to your own goal). Used as a counter when the opponent expects "over." |

## ✅ Desired Outcomes

- Every player can communicate using correct terminology during live play
- Film sessions are more productive because players understand positional language
- New players can get up to speed quickly by studying this reference
- Coaches and players share a common vocabulary for faster teaching and adjustments

---

## 📋 Coaching Points

- **Repetition builds fluency:** Quiz players on terms during warm-ups and water breaks
- **Use terms in practice:** Coaches should use proper terminology at all times — never "that area over there"
- **Film reinforcement:** Pause film and ask players to name the dodge, the slide, or the field area
- **New player onboarding:** Hand this glossary to every new player on day one
