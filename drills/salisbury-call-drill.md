# 🥍 Salisbury Call Drill

**Video Reference:** [Salisbury Call Drill | Lacrosse | POWLAX](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=jUlHAUToyhE)

## 🎯 Overview

The Salisbury Call Drill is a defensive slide and recovery drill. It focuses on teaching the defense how to react when an offensive player dodges down the alley, specifically working on the first slide from the crease, the second slide, and the recovery of the beaten defender.

**Target Audience:** This drill is primarily for **defense**, focusing on slide packages and recoveries.

## 💡 Purpose

The primary purpose of this drill is to teach **defense** how to handle specific slide and recovery scenarios:

1. **First Slide:** How to slide from the crease when the offense is dodging down the alley.
2. **Second Slide:** How to be the second slide from the crease to support the first slide.
3. **Recovery:** How the defensive middie up top needs to recover back into the crease after they are beat.

## ⚙️ Setup

- **Location:** Half-field setup, utilizing the box area and crease.
- **Players:**
  - **Offense:** A dodger up top (usually a midfielder) and players on the wings/crease to occupy the defense.
  - **Defense:** A defender on the ball up top, a crease defender (first slide), and another backside/crease defender (second slide).
- **Starting Positions:** The drill starts with an offensive player up top initiating a dodge down the alley against a defensive midfielder.

## 🏃 Execution

1. **The Dodge:** The offensive player up top dodges hard down the alley, intentionally beating the defensive midfielder.
2. **The First Slide:** As the dodger gets a step, the crease defender must recognize the threat and execute the **first slide** to stop the ball carrier.
3. **The Second Slide:** Simultaneously, the backside defender must drop in to cover the crease, executing the **second slide** to replace the first slider.
4. **The Recovery:** The defensive midfielder who was beaten up top must immediately turn and **recover** by sprinting back into the crease area to pick up the open man or find the new assignment.
5. **The Finish:** The drill can end with a shot, a pass to the crease, or simply when the defense successfully executes the slide, second slide, and recovery, neutralizing the threat.

## 🧠 What Players Can Expect

- **Quick Decision Making ⚡:** Defenders must read the play instantly and communicate their slides.
- **Communication 🗣️:** Loud, clear calls are essential (e.g., "Fire!", "Two!", "I'm in!").
- **Hustle on Recovery 🏃‍♂️:** The beaten defender cannot give up on the play; they must sprint to the "hole" (crease).

## ✅ Desired Outcomes

- **Improved Slide Timing:** Defenders learn exactly when to leave their man to stop the ball.
- **Seamless Second Slides:** The defense learns to rotate as a unit, ensuring the most dangerous area (the crease) is always covered.
- **Relentless Recovery:** Instills the habit of recovering to the middle of the field after getting beat, preventing easy dump passes to the crease.

## 📋 Coaching Points

- **Slide with Purpose:** The first slide must arrive under control but with enough presence to stop the dodger's momentum.
- **Head on a Swivel 👀:** The second slider must see both the ball and the man they are dropping to cover.
- **Find the Hole:** The recovering defender must sprint directly to the crease first, then find their man, rather than chasing the ball.

## 🔄 Variations

### Adding a Relay Player (7:32 Mark)

To expand on this drill and give the offense reps at moving the ball after drawing a slide, you can add a **relay player** (or a coach) in the middle of the field up top.

1. **The Dodge & Roll Away:** The offensive midfielder dodges down the alley, accepts the slide, and then rolls away (using a question mark or wide turn).
2. **The Throwback:** Instead of forcing a pass forward, the dodger throws the ball back up top to the relay player.
3. **The Swing:** The relay player immediately moves the ball to the next dodger in line on the opposite side of the field.
4. **Continuous Flow:** The new dodger attacks the opposite alley, forcing the defense to reset, communicate, and execute their slide package from the other side.

This variation is excellent for teaching offensive players how to step away from pressure and keep the ball moving, while forcing the defense to handle continuous, side-to-side slide scenarios.
